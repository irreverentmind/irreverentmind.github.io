What a faker!



When we think about how much of our lives we devote to getting people to like us, well…
presentation of self IS the authentic self?

Hard work, that.
 
Of course at some level we know it’s not real; we know we’re putting on a false front.

We know we’re often not interested in whether some stranger is having a good morning.

We know we’re often not smart, not nice, and god knows we’re nowhere near as together as we pretend to be all day long.
 
Our only interest is in fooling others to see us as what we are not.
 
And if others do fall for our marketing, if they do buy it, we’re momentarily satisfied until the unease sneaks back in.
 
Because every compliment comes with a sense that we fooled them.
 
Don’t get me wrong; we prefer the feeling of fooling them better than not fooling them.
 
But we know we’ve been fake and image-managing, because moments after getting the review we want, we are embarrassed, uncomfortable, dissatisfied, and wanting more.
 
And what happens (shudder) when they don’t believe the façade we like?
 
Hello shame and humiliation.
 
We hate when people don’t fall for the fake front.

We’re ashamed that we’ve been caught and that our pretense didn’t work.
 
We don’t want to be seen for the frauds that we are.
 
That’s why we get upset with people who don’t think we’re smart or wonderful or cute.
 
“I try so hard! What do I have to do to get people to think I’m great?”
 
This is what all reaction, manipulation and approval-seeking is about.
 
We don’t like people who see us. We don’t want to be seen.

We work too hard for them to buy the story we like.

We work too hard to control the marketing message.
 
But maybe it's not a bad thing when they don’t like us or see us the preferred way, even though we don’t like it one bit.
 
Because what we’re protecting so hard… is. a. pretense.

It's fakery. Image. Not real.
 
So we’re living a lie. All the time. Never what we seem to be.
 
It feels false because it is false.
 
So could it be that it’s not possible to be human and not be a fraud?
 
Could it be that posing as what we are not is just what comes with this human thing?
 
Then if it’s not possible to be human and not be a fake, maybe instead of trying to cover up fakery, we can know, at least internally, that the “real me” whatever that is, is never going to be presentable as a package.
 
Because it can’t be packaged.
 
So seeing that, maybe we can just relax in to what human really is.
 
An honest, genuine fake.
 
In which case maybe it's okay to let even a fake “good morning” be enough.
 
Without shame or humiliation or self loathing for being phony.
 
Whew.
 
Sooo much less work.

When we think about how much of our lives are devoted to getting people to think about us in a way we like, well…

It’s a lot, isn’t it?
 
A whole lot.
 
We want people to see us as smart, kind, calm, happy, evolved, knowledgeable, attractive, good, successful, real, unaffected, humble.

Or maybe we’d rather be seen as cool, wild, fearless, not caring what people think, not like others, not a rule follower.
 
Either way, the list goes on and on.
 
Watch how much effort goes into maneuvering others into getting the self-approved idea of us.
 
Notice the packaging, the presentation management going on. 
 
We’re constantly making sure the package looks right, sounds right, does the right thing, doesn’t look too sad or scared or troubled.
 
We’re constantly putting “lipstick on that pig,"

Polishing the façade.
 
It’s marketing. 
 
And this management of the image pervades everything, doesn’t it?
 
Even when we do something as insignificant as saying, “Good morning” to someone, we’re reviewing…

“Did I sound like I meant it? Did I seem friendly, pleasantly interested in them, not too stressed?”
 
Notice the word “seem?”
 
We are constantly reviewing the presentation to see if we are fooling others into believing we're all that.
 
All while also looking like we’re just being “authentic” and natural and not trying to fool. 
 
We even have to hide and manage the fact that we’re hiding and managing.
